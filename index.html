The Route

Darwin → Katherine Gorge (1–2 nights) → Central Arnhem Road → Weemol/Bulman (2–3 nights, Mimal work) → Nhulunbuy/Yirrkala (2 nights) → return west → Kakadu (Ubirr · Jim Jim · Gunlom · Maguk) → Darwin. Roughly circular, all within the Top End. Maningrida is not accessible by road for non-local visitors — confirmed.

🏞

Katherine Gorge — the opener

▾

Nitmiluk (Katherine Gorge) is one of the NT's great natural wonders and only 300 km from Darwin — a perfect first night with the boys. The gorge system has 13 interconnected gorges carved through sandstone. In June the water levels are perfect for swimming in the lower gorges. The 2-gorge walk (8.4 km, moderate) is excellent for Lewis at 10; Reuben (now 7) will need a hand on some sections but is totally doable. Canoe hire is another option — paddle into the gorge system.

Nitmiluk Centre is excellent for Indigenous cultural context before heading into Arnhem Land — the Jawoyn people's connection to Country is presented beautifully and sets the tone for what's ahead.

🗺

Central Arnhem Road — what to expect

▾

Distance: Katherine to Nhulunbuy is approximately 729 km, with the sealed section ending around 52 km from Katherine. The rest is formed gravel — variable quality with corrugations, bull dust pockets and some water crossings.

Speed: 80 km/h max recommended on the unsealed sections. At 12L/100km you'll consume more at speed. Budget 8–10 hours driving time Katherine to Nhulunbuy, split over 2 days with an overnight at Mainoru or a gravel pit.

Fuel: Available at Wulugarr/Beswick (~60 km from Stuart Hwy), Mainoru Store (~197 km), and Bulman (~256 km). Nhulunbuy has a BP with 24/7 diesel. With your 120L ARB tank + 40L jerry cans, you have 160L total — more than enough to cover any section.

Trailers: The NLC's official position is no caravans, but off-road camper trailers are a different category. The road has been driven by Cub Escape and similar trailers. Confirm with NLC when applying for your permit. Road conditions in June are at their best.

🎨

Yirrkala + Nhulunbuy — cultural heart

▾

Yirrkala is one of the most significant places in Australian Aboriginal art history — the Yirrkala Church Panels (1962) were Australia's first formal petition by Aboriginal people to Parliament. The Buku-Larrŋgay Mulka Centre is a world-class art centre and museum, open Mon–Fri 8am–4:30pm, Sat 9am–12pm. For the boys, seeing bark paintings and listening to the stories behind them in their Country of origin is a completely different experience to seeing them in a Darwin gallery.

Nhulunbuy is a real town — Woolworths, a hospital, pub, accommodation. Stock up here before heading back west. East Arnhem Land is a dry area; plan ahead with any beverages before arrival.

Note on Chris's SSP work: Given your Mimal partnership, arriving in Yirrkala with that context will enrich the visit enormously. Consider whether any of your research contacts could facilitate an introduction to local rangers or artists while you're there — Lewis and Reuben would benefit greatly.

🐊

Kakadu — the return leg

▾

Maningrida is not accessible by road for visitors. The community is accessible by air or barge only — the track between Maningrida and Ramingining is closed to all non-local traffic. It's simply not on the table for this trip. Drop it with no regret — you have more than enough on this route.

Kakadu on the return leg is focused entirely on swimming and rock art — no tours, just the best swimming holes in the park. Maguk (Barramundi Gorge) is a stunning waterfall into a deep clear pool. Gunlom has tiered falls and the famous infinity pools at the top with views across the escarpment. Jim Jim Falls (4WD track, 50 km unsealed) has the most dramatic plunge pool in Kakadu — Lewis and Reuben at 10 and 7 will love the boulder scramble to reach it. Ubirr at sunset is non-negotiable. The Bowali Visitor Centre has excellent hands-on displays for kids that tie together everything they've seen in Arnhem Land.

Vehicle — Same Rig, New Terrain

Same Ford Everest + Cub Escape as the Gibb trip. Most of your prep carries over. Key differences for the Central Arnhem Road are noted below.

✓
Your rig is well proven after the Gibb. The Central Arnhem Road is rougher in places (more bull dust, less maintained than the Gibb) but shorter overall. The 120L ARB tank + 40L jerry cans is if anything even more useful here — fuel points are more spread out. All the gear from the Gibb applies directly.

What's different vs the Gibb

  • Bull dust pockets — more prevalent on the Central Arnhem Road than the Gibb. Approach at lower speed (50–60 km/h) — hitting bull dust at speed causes sudden heavy steering pull and can cause a spin. Your suspension handles it but respect the conditions.
  • Water crossings — several remain even in dry season. The Wilton River near Bulman and Goyder River crossings now have bridges. Check NT Road Report before departure for any unexpected closures.
  • Fuel gaps are wider — Stuart Hwy turnoff to Nhulunbuy is ~729 km with fuel at Beswick (~60 km), Mainoru (~197 km), Bulman (~256 km, NLC permit needed to enter community), and Nhulunbuy BP. With 160L total capacity you can cover the full road without stress but top up at every opportunity.
  • Speed limit — 80 km/h maximum recommended on Central Arnhem Road unsealed sections. At 80 km/h vs 120 km/h your fuel consumption drops significantly — expect 9–10L/100km on the gravel rather than 12L.
  • Road trains — regular community freight deliveries. Pass carefully, pull right off the road to let them through, wait for the dust to clear before moving.
  • Remote recovery — the Central Arnhem Road has far fewer travellers than the Gibb. Your satellite comms (iPhone 17 Pro Max + PLB) are more critical here. Leave a detailed trip plan with a Darwin contact including expected overnight locations and check-in points (Mainoru has phone signal, Nhulunbuy obviously).

Tyre Pressures — Central Arnhem Road

  • Air down before the unsealed section (Stuart Hwy turnoff) — 28–30 PSI with the Cub Escape loaded
  • Bull dust sections: keep pressure up slightly (30–32 PSI) for better steering control
  • Reinflate before Nhulunbuy — there are sealed sections on the Gove Peninsula
  • Reinflate fully before Kakadu Highway return — sealed road to Pine Creek and Darwin

Cahills Crossing — specific vehicle prep

  • Cahill's Crossing is approximately 300 mm deep at low tide — well within Everest's wading depth
  • Check tide times before approaching (NT Government tidal data online)
  • Walk the crossing before driving — check depth, substrate, and confirm no crocodile presence at the crossing point
  • Cross in low range, steady pace — don't stop
  • The Cub Escape will get wet — pack accordingly (electronics in dry bags)
